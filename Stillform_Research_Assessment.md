## THE VERDICT UP FRONT

Stillform is built on individually validated components — breathing, cognitive reappraisal, affect labeling, body awareness — that each have published evidence behind them. The combination is novel and deeper than any competing app. But the honest truth is: **no one has tested whether combining them this way produces better outcomes than any single component alone.** The app hasn't been clinically validated, the AI hasn't been studied, and the biggest risk isn't whether the tools work — it's whether people keep using them.

## WHY IT SHOULD WORK

### 1. Breathing Regulation — Strong Immediate Evidence

Paced breathing consistently produces measurable physiological changes: increased HRV, parasympathetic activation, reduced cortisol. Even single 2-minute sessions produce detectable shifts (Magnon et al. 2021; Pozzato et al. 2025). Longitudinal studies show sustained improvements in resting HRV with ongoing practice (Jung et al. 2024).

A 2025 narrative review of breathing research found that "slow, nasal and/or diaphragmatic breathing consistently demonstrated enhanced autonomic regulation via vagal innervation" across both short and long study durations. A 2024 systematic review of 58 clinical trials found 54 of 72 breathing interventions were effective for stress/anxiety reduction.

**Stillform application:** Four breathing patterns with visual pacing. The research supports this.

### 2. Emotion Regulation Apps Show Medium Effect Sizes

A 2021 meta-analysis of 39 RCTs on mental health apps (Eisenstadt et al., JMIR Mental Health) found:
- Small effect for reducing mental health symptoms (g = -0.24)
- Small effect for improving well-being (g = 0.17)
- **Medium effect for emotion regulation (g = 0.49)**

Emotion regulation — what Stillform is specifically built for — showed the strongest effect of any outcome measured. Most apps in the review focused on symptom management, not regulation. Stillform targets the mechanism that works best.

### 3. AI Chatbots Reduce Symptoms in Short-Term

A 2024 meta-analysis of 18 RCTs involving 3,477 participants found AI chatbots produced "noteworthy improvements in depression (g = -0.26) and anxiety (g = -0.19)." Woebot, Wysa, and Youper all showed reductions in depression and anxiety with high user engagement. A 2025 study of AI-driven chatbots for young people found a "moderate-to-large effect (g = 0.61)" on depressive symptoms after adjusting for publication bias.

**Stillform application:** GPT-4o with 9 context variables per call — deeper personalization than any chatbot in the literature.

### 4. Affect Labeling Is Robustly Supported

Lieberman et al. (2007) demonstrated with fMRI that simply naming an emotion reduces amygdala activation and increases prefrontal cortex engagement. This has been replicated across multiple studies, populations, and modalities. A 2024 fNIRS study confirmed that affect labeling activates the right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex and suppresses emotion processing circuits. The effect is automatic — it works even without conscious intent to regulate.

**Stillform application:** 18 distinct emotion chips on the Pulse screen. This IS affect labeling.

### 5. Cognitive Reappraisal Has Decades of Evidence

Ochsner, Gross, and colleagues have published extensively showing that reframing how you think about a situation changes the emotional response. Neuroimaging confirms reappraisal engages prefrontal regulatory circuits and dampens amygdala reactivity (Buhle et al. 2014 meta-analysis).

**Stillform application:** The Reframe AI's primary function is guided reappraisal.

### 6. The Combination Is Novel

Most apps do one thing: meditation (Calm, Headspace), breathing (Breathe2Relax), or CBT chatbot (Woebot, Wysa). No published app combines:
- Body-first regulation (breathing + body scan)
- Cognitive reappraisal via personalized AI
- Physical state filtering (bio-filter)
- Affect labeling (emotion chips)
- Interpersonal bias detection
- Daily bookend structure (morning + EOD check-in)

This integration hasn't been studied, but the individual components have strong evidence.

## WHY IT MIGHT NOT WORK

### 1. The Biggest Breathing Study Found No Difference vs Placebo

The largest RCT on breathing to date (Fincham et al. 2023, Scientific Reports, n=400) compared coherent breathing at 5.5 breaths/min against a placebo at 12 breaths/min. Both groups improved significantly. **There was no difference between groups.** The specific breathing pattern may not matter — just paying attention to your breath seems to be enough.

**What this means for Stillform:** The four breathing patterns (Regulate, Box, 4-7-8, Quick Reset) may all work equally well — not because they're scientifically distinct, but because focused attention on breath is the active ingredient. The choice gives users agency, which may matter psychologically even if the pattern itself doesn't matter physiologically.

**The honest framing:** Don't oversell specific patterns. The value is in the practice, not the ratio.

### 2. AI Chatbot Effects Don't Last

The same 2024 meta-analysis that found chatbot benefits at 8 weeks also found: **"at the three-month follow-up, no substantial effects were detected for either condition."** The improvements appear to be temporary. Qualitative feedback from participants reported "feeling relaxed during and after sessions, but not experiencing enduring, long-term change."

**What this means for Stillform:** The Reframe AI may help in the moment but not produce lasting cognitive change. The session feels good but the rewiring may not stick.

**The counterargument:** Stillform's AI learns over time (session notes, pattern recognition, escalating coaching). No chatbot in the literature does this. The personalization may sustain effects longer — but this hasn't been tested.

### 3. Retention Is the Existential Threat

Real-world data is brutal:
- **30-day retention for mental health apps: 3.3%** (study of 93 Android apps)
- **Clinical trial dropout: 26.2%** — rises to **47.8%** when adjusted for publication bias
- **77% of users stop using an app after a single use** (industry data)

A 2025 meta-analysis of 79 RCTs found that gamification **did not improve and might weaken retention**. This validates Stillform's anti-gamification stance. What DID help: reminders and human contact.

**What this means for Stillform:** The daily reminder, morning check-in, and EOD check-in are exactly the structure that research says reduces dropout. But without human support (therapist, coach, community), retention will be a constant battle. The Neuro voice companion — if built — could partially address the "human contact" factor that research shows matters most.

### 4. Self-Guided Apps Produce Weaker Effects Than Guided Ones

Apps with professional guidance (therapist review, human feedback) consistently show lower dropout and larger effect sizes. Stillform is fully self-guided. A 2025 meta-analysis found that "features that support accountability, including reminders and human contact, reduce dropout."

**What this means for Stillform:** Consider adding optional "check-in from a real person" as a premium feature post-launch. Even a weekly text from a human coach could dramatically improve retention and outcomes.

### 5. The AI Hasn't Been Clinically Validated

A 2025 systematic review of 160 AI chatbot studies found that only 16% of LLM-based chatbots underwent clinical efficacy testing, with 77% still in early validation. Stillform's GPT-4o Reframe has zero published evidence. The system prompts are thoughtful and well-designed — but no one has measured whether they produce better outcomes than a simple CBT worksheet.

**What this means for Stillform:** You can cite the research behind the principles (reappraisal, affect labeling) but you cannot claim the AI itself is clinically effective. Keep language around "built on proven research" not "clinically proven."

### 6. Momentary Relief vs Lasting Change

A consistent pattern across breathing, chatbot, and app research: immediate effects are real, long-term effects are uncertain. Breathing reduces anxiety in the moment. The AI helps in the session. But whether these translate to lasting neuroplasticity, changed default responses, or reduced baseline reactivity over months — the evidence is thin.

**What this means for Stillform:** The pre/post rating shift is real. Whether it accumulates into lasting change depends on consistent daily practice. The stress inoculation model (Meichenbaum) supports this — but only if users keep showing up. Which brings us back to retention.

## WHAT GIVES STILLFORM A GENUINE EDGE

1. **Bio-filter is genuinely novel.** No published app checks physical state before interpreting emotional state. "I feel overwhelmed" + "I'm depleted" = different intervention than "I feel overwhelmed" + "I'm all clear." This is the misattribution of arousal applied in real-time. Nobody else does this.

2. **Anti-gamification aligns with the research.** The meta-analysis found gamification doesn't help retention and may hurt it. Stillform's refusal to add streaks, points, and badges is supported by evidence, not just philosophy.

3. **Emotion chip granularity is supported.** Barrett's research specifically shows that people who distinguish between 12 emotions regulate better than people who only distinguish "good" vs "bad." The 18-chip Pulse system is granularity training in disguise.

4. **Three-pathway integration is unique.** Body (breathing + scan), thought (AI reframe), and signal (affect labeling + bio-filter) in one session flow. Most apps are single-modality.

5. **The composure-when-winning framing has no competition.** Every mental health app targets crisis or negative states. Stillform is the only one that explicitly addresses overcommitment, blind spots during high energy, and impulsive decisions when things are going well.

6. **Daily bookend structure matches retention research.** Morning check-in + evening close creates routine. Research shows reminders and structured check-ins are the strongest retention drivers.

## THE RISKS TO NAME HONESTLY

1. **No clinical validation.** Everything is theoretical until tested on real users with measurable outcomes.

2. **Single point of failure.** One developer (AI-assisted). One codebase. No native apps yet. No backup infrastructure.

3. **Privacy model is honor-system.** localStorage + client-side encryption. No server-side verification. No HIPAA compliance (not required, but users may assume it).

4. **AI dependency risk.** If users rely on the AI instead of developing internal regulation skills, the app becomes a crutch, not scaffolding.

5. **Small target market.** Neurodivergent, high-intensity people who hate meditation — how big is this? It may be bigger than expected, but it's not "everyone."

6. **Founder capacity.** Neurological disability affecting cognitive function. Building a product that requires consistent development, marketing, and user support.

## WHAT TO DO WITH THIS INFORMATION

**Don't change the product.** The components are sound. The combination is novel. The philosophy is honest.

**Do these things:**

1. **Track the right metrics from day one.** Pre/post shifts, time-to-regulation, sessions per week, 7/14/30 day retention. These are your clinical proxies until you can run a real study.

2. **Never say "clinically proven."** Say "built on proven neuroscience research." Cite the individual components. Don't claim the combination is validated until it is.

3. **Plan for retention as the primary challenge.** The daily check-in structure is your best weapon. The Neuro voice companion could be your second. A future "human check-in" tier could be your third.

4. **Get 100 users and measure.** The first real test isn't Reddit upvotes — it's whether 10 of those 100 are still using the app at day 30. If they are, you have something. If they aren't, the research tells you why.

5. **Consider a partnership with a researcher.** A graduate student in clinical psychology or neuroscience could run a pilot study on Stillform for their thesis. It costs you nothing and gives you published evidence.

6. **The "unfolding" question is real but unmeasurable right now.** Focus on the measurable: did they regulate? Did they come back? Did their baseline improve? The unfolding — the person discovering who they are under the scaffolding — will show up in qualitative data from real users. That's your testimonial strategy.
