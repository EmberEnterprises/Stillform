# STILLFORM FRAMING LAW
**ARA Embers LLC · May 12, 2026 · v1.0**

The single source of truth for what Stillform IS, what it IS NOT, and what the product produces. This doc is read every session before any feature work, content draft, or external communication. Banned defaults documented here override pattern-match defaults from training data or other docs.

If a proposal, prompt, or piece of copy contradicts this doc, the proposal is wrong and this doc is right. The audit philosophy's Framing Layer (Layer 0) passes/fails against this doc.

## WHAT STILLFORM IS

Stillform is a **metacognition practice**. Users build rich, specific mental representations of their own internal states, patterns, triggers, and reactions — the concept library the brain uses to perceive itself. Through repeated analytical practice, this library becomes more numerous, more granular, more accessible. Neuroplastic change accrues: the analytical capacity becomes fast enough that pattern recognition feels like direct perception.

The result is **cognitive expansion**: sharper thinking, better strategic decisions, deeper self-understanding. Composure is one of the felt outcomes that signals the practice is working.

Stillform is AI-assisted. The AI is the practice partner. It structures the analytical work, holds the boundaries of each session, and helps the user reach a takeaway that adds to the concept library. It does not regulate them, calm them, or fix them.

## WHAT STILLFORM IS NOT

Stillform is NOT:
- A self-regulation app
- A regulation app
- A composure app
- A wellness app
- A meditation app
- A crisis tool
- A therapy substitute
- A tool for "intense people"
- A tool for distress relief
- A tool for "calming down"
- A tool that "helps you regulate"
- A "just observe" contemplative tool (the audience needs analytical structure, not silence)
- A rumination-inducing introspection tool (see "Failure Modes of the Practice")
- An open-ended journaling app (the metacognition practice is bounded, not open-ended)

Regulation, composure, calm — these are **felt outcomes** users may experience as RESULTS of the practice. They are NOT what the product sells. The product is the metacognitive practice itself; the outcomes are evidence the practice is working.

Positioning Stillform as a regulation/composure tool puts a ceiling on the product at "back to baseline." Stillform raises the baseline. That distinction is non-negotiable.

## REGULATION AND COMPOSURE WITHIN THE PRACTICE

Regulation and composure are not the product. They are functional elements that aid the metacognition practice. They have an upstream role (cutting noise so the practice can land) AND a downstream role (felt outcomes that signal the practice worked).

**The upstream role: cutting noise.**

Regulation tools — breath work, body scan, somatic interventions — serve a specific function within the practice. They cut the noise that would otherwise crowd out metacognitive work:
- Physiological activation (heart rate, muscle tension, breath shortening)
- Cognitive intrusion (rumination, anxiety loops, racing thoughts)
- Sensory overload
- Bodily discomfort pulling attention

Reducing this noise raises the signal-to-noise ratio in favor of attending to one's own processing. The user CAN'T notice how they process if the noise is too loud. Regulation tools clear the channel.

**The downstream role: felt outcomes.**

Composure is what users notice after the practice has worked. They feel steadier, sharper, more themselves. This felt outcome serves a second function: it enables deeper iterations of the practice. A composed user can engage in more advanced metacognitive work than an activated one. So composure is both a result AND an aid to the next round.

**The practice and its aids feed each other in an upward spiral.**

User practices → some composure achieved → deeper practice becomes possible → more composure → even deeper practice. This compounding is what produces cognitive expansion over time. It's why the product compounds with reps — neuroplasticity from sustained practice, not single-session relief.

**The distinguishing rule:**

A feature that USES regulation to support the practice IS Stillform. A feature that IS regulation in place of the practice is NOT Stillform.

- Breathe before a Reframe session → uses regulation to support practice ✓
- Breathe as a standalone "calm down" tool with no link to metacognition → IS regulation, not Stillform ✗
- Body Scan that ends with a metacognitive naming chip → uses regulation to support practice ✓
- Body Scan that ends with "Signal cleared" and sends user home without metacognitive integration → IS regulation, not Stillform ✗

This is the audit question for every feature: does the regulation here serve the practice, or replace it?

## THE AUDIENCE

Stillform is for people who want to **ENHANCE** themselves. They:
- Treat the mind as a trainable system
- Want sharper thinking, not relief
- Want to understand HOW they process, not have someone else handle their processing
- Are often analytical by nature — they think in patterns, structures, systems
- Are operators, founders, high-functioning users — people already functioning who want to function better
- Came to Stillform to get smarter, not to get calm

They are NOT:
- People in active distress seeking immediate relief
- People with clinical conditions seeking treatment
- People who want to be told they're OK
- People who want to feel better without doing the practice work

This audience is the design constraint. Features that serve "people in distress" without also serving "people enhancing themselves" violate the framing. Because this audience is analytical by inclination, the analytical substance of the practice is a feature, not a bug — it matches how they already think.

## THE MECHANISM

Stillform produces results through:
1. **Metacognitive practice** — structured analytical work that builds the concept library the brain uses to perceive its own states. Repeated over time, analysis becomes fast and granular enough that pattern recognition feels like direct perception.
2. **Neuroplasticity** — the brain physically changes with practice
3. **Cognitive skill acquisition** — emotional granularity, interoceptive accuracy, strategic decision-making, pattern recognition

Supported by:
- **Regulation tools** (breath work, body scan, somatic interventions) — these cut noise so the practice can land. They serve the mechanism, they are not the mechanism. See "Regulation and Composure Within the Practice" above.

NOT through:
- Calming techniques used as ends in themselves
- Affirmations
- Coping mechanisms unmoored from metacognitive work
- Crisis interventions

The mechanism IS the product. Regulation tools support the mechanism. When a feature loses sight of the mechanism — when it becomes calming-as-end-in-itself, unmoored from the practice — it has drifted out of Stillform.

## WHAT THE PRACTICE LOOKS LIKE

Metacognition in Stillform is **fundamentally analytical work** — building rich, specific mental representations of one's own internal states, patterns, triggers, and reactions. Observation in mature practice is not analysis-free; it is analysis operating on a deep concept library at high speed, feeling like direct perception.

This is the crucial framing: **the practice doesn't graduate from analytical to observational.** It deepens analytical capacity until analysis itself becomes fast, granular, and effortless. The "observational" experience is what high-skill analysis looks like from the inside.

**Why analysis is the substance, not the scaffolding:**

The brain perceives internal states by constructing them using concepts (Barrett 2017 — constructed emotion theory). You cannot perceive what you don't have concepts for. A user with only "stressed" perceives everything as stress. A user with thirty differentiated emotional and cognitive concepts perceives nuance.

Stillform's analytical tools — the bias profiler (10 cognitive distortions), the trigger profile, the pulse log + chips, the signal log, the Reframe sessions — are **concept-building tools**. They literally construct the mental representations the brain uses to perceive itself. The analytical work IS the rewiring. Take it away and there is nothing for "observation" to operate on.

This is why "just observe" approaches fail Stillform's audience. People who came to understand their own processing — often neurodivergent, often intensely cognitive, often analytical by nature — need structured analytical work. They do not need to be taught to stop thinking. They need analytical work that produces insight rather than rumination.

**The developmental arc — same mechanism, increasing speed:**

- **Early practice** — concept-building. Users learn the framework (10 distortions), identify their specific triggers, build vocabulary for their emotional states. Slow, effortful, explicit. Each Reframe walks through analysis step by step. The user is consciously constructing self-knowledge.

- **Sustained practice** — concept-deepening. The framework becomes familiar. The user has personal data — patterns they've named, triggers they've identified, reactions they've reframed. Analysis runs faster because the concepts are richer and more accessible.

- **Mature practice** — analytical pattern recognition operating at the speed of perception. The user senses a state arising and identifies it almost simultaneously. The bias they're operating under is named at a glance. This is NOT analysis-free awareness — it is analysis so fast and concept-rich it feels like direct seeing. The neuroplastic change has built analytical capacity into the substrate of perception itself.

Users do not skip stages. The slow, explicit analytical work in early practice IS what produces the speed and granularity of mature practice. Stillform serves all three stages with the same mechanism — concept-building tools — adjusted for where the user is in the arc.

**What productive practice looks like at any stage:**

- **Naming with specificity.** "I'm anxious about the pitch" not "I feel bad." Emotional granularity (Barrett 2001; Kashdan, Barrett, McKnight 2015; Hoemann et al. 2021) is the trainable capacity at the practice's core. The more specific the naming, the richer the concept library, the more nuanced future perception becomes.

- **Bounded engagements.** Each analytical pass has a START, a SCOPE, and a CLOSE. The user analyzes a specific pattern, reaches a takeaway, closes the loop. Ecological Momentary Intervention methodology (Myin-Germeys et al. 2016, 2018) — brief, in-context, repeated. Stillform IS an EMI.

- **Generative, not circular.** Each analytical pass produces NEW understanding — a fresh observation, a clearer pattern, a more precise name. The output is an addition to the concept library. Same ground rehashed without new insight = rumination, not concept-building.

- **Curious, not judging.** Analysis from "what's actually here?" not "what's wrong with me?" The judging move turns concept-building into self-criticism.

- **Closure after each engagement.** Naming, noticing, takeaway, release. Stillform's closing rituals ("Composure restored," "Signal cleared," "Pattern observed") signal the loop is closed — this turn is over, the concept has been added, not subject to further mining.

**What the practice IS NOT, at any stage:**

- **Looping on the same ground without new insight.** The concept is already built; further passes don't add to the library = rumination.

- **Unbounded introspection.** Open-ended "let me think about myself" with no scope, no question, no exit = stage-1 meta-control failure (Hitchcock et al. 2024).

- **Self-judgment dressed as analysis.** "Why am I always like this?" is not analysis — it's a conclusion that masquerades as inquiry.

- **Analysis without a closure ritual.** Sessions that don't end. Journaling without bounds. Re-reading past entries searching for hidden meaning. These features train the rumination loop, not the concept library.

The audit question is NOT "is this analytical or observational?" Both are the same mechanism at different stages of maturity. The audit question is: **"Does this engagement BUILD, REFINE, or APPLY a concept — bounded and generatively? Or does it loop on already-built concepts without producing new insight (rumination)?"**

## FAILURE MODES OF THE PRACTICE

Metacognition can produce rumination as easily as it produces insight. Stillform's design must specifically guard against the rumination failure mode — which is NOT "analysis" itself, but a SPECIFIC FAILURE PATTERN within analysis.

The distinction matters because Stillform's audience NEEDS analytical work — they came to understand their own processing. Telling them "just observe, don't analyze" fails the entire user base. The protection isn't against analysis; it's against the structural failure pattern that turns analysis into rumination.

**The structural pattern of rumination:**

- **Unbounded** — no scope, no exit criteria, no closing ritual
- **Looping** — same ground covered repeatedly without new insight
- **Circular** — analysis returns to the same conclusion (usually self-blame or hopelessness)
- **Open-ended** — no specific question, just "let me think about myself"
- **Judgment-coded** — analysis FROM a position of "I'm broken" rather than "I'm curious"

A productive analytical session has the opposite structure: bounded scope, specific question, generative output, closure ritual, curious framing.

**Wells (2009) metacognitive model.** Depression and anxiety are maintained by metacognitive beliefs that RUMINATION specifically is helpful — "if I think about this enough, I'll figure it out." The belief drives repeated thinking; the thinking strengthens the disorder. Critically, Wells doesn't say analysis is the problem. He distinguishes:
- Type 1 worry / cognitive analysis (about external problems or internal patterns) — can be productive
- Type 2 worry / meta-worry (worry ABOUT worry, rumination ABOUT rumination) — the maintenance loop

Stillform protects against Type 2: features that invite meta-analysis of one's own internal patterns ("am I ruminating right now?", "what does it mean that I'm worried about this?") create the maintenance loop.

**Hitchcock et al. (2024) meta-control account.** Rumination and worry are coarse terms for "failure in meta-control" — like tripping and falling are coarse terms for failure in motor control. Four meta-control stages can fail: open-ended thoughts (stage 1), subgoal execution (stage 2), switching (stage 3), and learning adaptive mental behavior (stage 4).

Stage 1 is the design-relevant one for Stillform: prompts that invite open-ended thinking without subgoal structure (specific question, defined scope, exit criteria) increase stage-1 failure probability. The fix is not to remove analysis — it's to STRUCTURE it.

**For Stillform's design, this means:**

- **Bound every analytical engagement.** Specific question (not "what's going on?"), defined scope (one pattern or trigger), exit criteria (named takeaway or "I don't know yet" close), time limit (sessions end). Open-ended is stage-1 failure.

- **Invite curiosity, not judgment.** "What's the most accurate name for this?" pulls toward generative analysis. "Why am I always like this?" pulls toward judgment loops. Same surface, opposite training.

- **Produce takeaways, not commentary.** Every engagement closes with a SPECIFIC insight, name, pattern, or move. "I learned X about myself" — generative. "I thought a lot" — circular.

- **Build closing rituals.** Sessions END. The practice is bounded. Closing language ("Composure restored," "Signal cleared," "Pattern observed") signals the loop is closed. This is one of the most important design constraints Stillform observes.

- **Limit AI engagement length.** Reframe sessions can't go indefinitely. The AI has a job: facilitate naming + a brief structured analysis + a closure. Open-ended chat with no exit point is rumination's natural environment.

- **Watch for meta-analysis affordances.** Features that show users their own pattern data ("you've felt anxious 8 days this week") risk Type 2 rumination — analyzing one's analysis. Use carefully; pair with bounded reframes, not standalone surfacing.

**The audit question for every Stillform surface:**

Does this STRUCTURE analysis so it stays bounded, specific, generative, and closure-bound? Or does it invite the unbounded, looping, circular, open-ended pattern that becomes rumination?

If the feature is bounded and generative — it serves the practice regardless of how analytical it is. If the feature is unbounded or open-ended — it trains rumination regardless of how "thoughtful" it appears.

## THE OUTCOMES

What users get from Stillform, in order of frequency-of-felt-experience:

1. **Composure** — felt outcome, what they notice fastest. Dual role: also feeds the next iteration of the practice (see "Regulation and Composure Within the Practice" above).
2. **Sharper thinking** — felt outcome, accrues over weeks
3. **Better strategic decisions** — felt outcome, accrues over weeks
4. **Deeper self-understanding** — felt outcome, accrues over months
5. **Emotional granularity** — cognitive skill, measurable, trainable
6. **Improved interoceptive accuracy** — cognitive skill, measurable, trainable
7. **Pattern recognition in one's own processing** — cognitive skill, measurable
8. **Neuroplastic change** — the underlying mechanism that produces all of the above

Composure is at the top of the list because it's what users notice FIRST. It is not the GOAL. The goal is the full stack. Marketing or product language that names ONLY composure violates the framing — it minimizes what the product actually delivers, AND it confuses the dual role (composure as outcome vs. composure as aid to further practice).

## THE SCIENCE FOUNDATION

Stillform's claims are grounded in literature across multiple fields. Regulation science is ONE component, not the foundation. Proposals citing only regulation literature have missed the spine.

**Metacognitive training (the spine):**
- Flavell (1979) on metacognitive monitoring
- Nelson & Narens (1990) on metacognitive frameworks
- Schraw & Moshman (1995) on metacognitive theories
- Veenman et al. (2006) on metacognition and learning
- Frontiers in Psychology (2026) — review synthesizing metacognition + neuroplasticity, naming prefrontal-parietal circuits, anterior cingulate cortex for monitoring, hippocampus for memory-related metacognition
- Education Endowment Foundation — metacognitive interventions produce up to seven months additional learning progress (one of the most effective educational strategies measured)

**Adaptive vs maladaptive metacognition** (the distinction Stillform's design must enforce):
- Wells (2009) — metacognitive model of psychopathology. Positive beliefs about rumination drive depression/anxiety.
- Hitchcock, Frank et al. (2024) — meta-control account of repetitive negative thinking. Rumination/worry as failure at one of four meta-control stages.
- These define what Stillform's practice must avoid. See "Failure Modes of the Practice" above.

**Emotional granularity** (the trainable skill at the practice's core):
- Barrett (2001) coined "emotional granularity" / "emotion differentiation"
- Kashdan, Barrett, McKnight (2015) — higher granularity → less maladaptive regulation, less neural reactivity to rejection, less severe anxiety/depression
- Hoemann et al. (2021) — experience sampling itself increases granularity. The act of repeatedly naming internal states IS the intervention. This is the science directly supporting Stillform's pulse log + chip system.
- Suvak et al. (2011) — granularity protects against impulsive self-regulation strategies
- Barrett (2017) — constructed emotion theory; emotion concepts as predictive structures the brain builds

**Ecological Momentary Intervention** (the methodological category):
- Myin-Germeys et al. (2016, 2018) — app-based interventions delivered in real-world contexts. Stillform IS an EMI. This is the methodological literature that grounds why brief, repeated, in-context practice works.

**Neuroplasticity from practice:**
- Davidson (Center for Healthy Minds) on contemplative neuroplasticity
- Lazar et al. (2005) on cortical thickening from meditation
- Calderone et al. (2024) — meditation produces measurable neural changes including increased cortical thickness and enhanced connectivity (more recent than Lazar)
- BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) as neuroplasticity mediator, with practice-induced expression supporting neurogenesis + synaptic plasticity (Frontiers 2026 review synthesis)

**Self-mastery psychological framework:**
- Bandura (1977) — self-efficacy theory. Mastery experience is the strongest source of self-efficacy beliefs. The practice IS a mastery experience.
- Pearlin (sense of mastery) — longitudinal evidence linking self-mastery to physical and mental health outcomes
- Performance psychology / cognitive fitness frameworks — high-performance cognition with periodization (assessment, training, augmentation, recovery)

**Deliberate practice / skill acquisition:**
- Ericsson (1993) on deliberate practice
- Ericsson & Pool (2016) "Peak"
- Klein on naturalistic decision-making and expert intuition

**Decision-making / cognitive bias** (what metacognition trains against):
- Kahneman (2011) "Thinking, Fast and Slow"
- Tversky & Kahneman on cognitive biases — the failure modes of fast cognition that metacognitive monitoring corrects

**Affect labeling** (one supporting mechanism, not the foundation):
- Lieberman (2007) — naming reduces amygdala reactivity in laboratory settings. Used as ONE supportive mechanism for the broader granularity practice, NOT as the primary citation.

**Cognitive reappraisal** (downstream act, requires some regulated baseline):
- Ochsner & Gross (2005) — restructuring meaning. Used after affect labeling has reduced reactivity enough for reappraisal to land.

**Selected regulation/somatic science** (in service of the spine, not the spine):
- Porges polyvagal — vagal activation supports parasympathetic engagement
- Gerritsen & Band (2018) — breath-based vagal activation
- Khalsa et al. (2018) — interoceptive awareness as foundation for emotion regulation
- Lehrer et al. (2020) — HRV biofeedback and breathing pace

The Framing Audit (audit philosophy Layer 0) fails proposals where:
1. Cited literature is regulation-only — the spine (metacognition + granularity + neuroplasticity) is missing
2. The proposed feature trains analytical metacognition rather than observational metacognition (see Failure Modes section)
3. The proposal doesn't articulate which trainable skill is being built (emotional granularity? interoceptive accuracy? metacognitive monitoring? decision-making under bias?)

Proposals must surface citations across the full spine AND demonstrate the proposed feature trains adaptive metacognition.
