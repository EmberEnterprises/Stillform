# STILLFORM WORKSHOP — INSTRUMENT #1: METACOGNITIVE BELIEFS

**ARA Embers LLC · spec locked May 17, 2026 · status: SPEC LOCKED, build pending**

Workshop instrument #1, adapted from the MCQ-30 (Wells & Cartwright-Hatton 2004). First instrument in the Workshop layer of Bias Profile (Phase 5 sub-item #4). Serves bias Domain 4 (metacognitive beliefs).

---

## 1 · WHAT THIS IS

The Workshop is the deeper-look layer of Bias Profile. Where the in-session and EOD discovery mechanisms surface patterns from the user's own language, the Workshop offers structured self-observation tools — each adapted from published research, rewritten into Stillform's own non-clinical voice. The result of each tool feeds back into the user's Bias Profile watch list.

Instrument #1 adapts the Metacognitions Questionnaire-30 (MCQ-30), the most Stillform-coherent instrument available because Stillform IS a metacognition practice. The MCQ-30 measures the beliefs a person holds *about their own thinking* — which is precisely the layer Stillform's practice operates on.

**This is not a clinical screen. It is not a diagnosis. It is a structured way for the user to observe the beliefs running beneath their own thinking.**

## 2 · SOURCE RESEARCH (integrity-through-research)

**Primary citation:** Wells, A., & Cartwright-Hatton, S. (2004). A short form of the metacognitions questionnaire: Properties of the MCQ-30. *Behaviour Research and Therapy*, 42(4), 385–396.

**Structure of the source instrument:** 30 items, 4-point agreement scale, five subscales of six items each. Validated repeatedly across community and clinical samples since 2004 (original community validation; subsequent validations in GAD, OCD, cancer-patient, and at-risk-psychosis populations; bifactor confirmation in a US community sample). Subscale internal consistency 0.84–0.93; total scale typically above 0.90. Widely translated.

The five subscales and the construct each measures:

1. **Positive Beliefs about Worry (POS)** — belief that worry is functional: helps prepare, cope, perform.
2. **Negative Beliefs about Uncontrollability and Danger (NEG)** — meta-belief that worry is uncontrollable AND damaging.
3. **(Lack of) Cognitive Confidence (CC)** — distrust of one's own memory and attention.
4. **Need to Control Thoughts (NC)** — belief that thoughts must be controlled; uncontrolled thoughts read as failure.
5. **Cognitive Self-Consciousness (CSC)** — tendency to monitor one's own thinking. (See §6 for the Stillform-specific reformulation of this subscale.)

**The verbatim MCQ-30 items are NOT reproduced in this spec or anywhere in Stillform.** They are copyrighted. Stillform's items are original adaptations faithful to each subscale's construct, written in Stillform's voice. Fidelity is to the *construct*, not the wording.

## 3 · ADAPTATION PRINCIPLES (from USER TREATMENT framing law)

Every item, response option, and result in this instrument holds to the USER TREATMENT principles (FRAMING_LAW v1.4 / CANON v1.8):

- **Non-clinical voice.** The science is the rigor; the voice is Stillform's. No clinical jargon, no pathology framing.
- **No scores shown.** The user never sees a number, a rank, or a good/bad judgment.
- **No prevalence framing.** Results never claim the pattern is common, widespread, typical, or rare. Research grounding comes from the model that names the pattern (Wells's metacognitive model), not from distribution claims.
- **Honesty paired with acceptance.** Results name what's running honestly. The voice softens delivery; it never softens substance. No hollow reassurance.
- **Every result has a path forward.** A named pattern always comes with a way to engage it (watch-list add + practice).

## 4 · THE 30 ADAPTED ITEMS

Grouped by subscale below for clarity. **In production, items interleave across subscales** (MCQ-30 standard practice — prevents the user from catching a subscale's theme and answering systematically).

### Workshop intro (shown before the user starts)

> This is a closer look at the beliefs that run beneath your thinking — not what you think, but how you relate to your own mind. The patterns named here come from a well-studied model of how thinking works. No scores, no right answers, no judgment. Read each statement; mark where it sits for you. About 5–10 minutes.

### POS — beliefs that worry is functional / useful
*Response options: Rings true · Sometimes · Not really · Haven't noticed*

1. Turning something over in my head before it happens helps me avoid problems later.
2. When something is hard, mental rehearsal helps me cope with it.
3. I need a level of worry running to perform at my best.
4. Turning something over in my head is how I sort it out — without that, I can't think clearly.
5. If I stopped worrying about things that matter, I'd be careless or something would slip.
6. The thinking I do under pressure is what produces the answer — without the pressure, I'd miss the solution.

### NEG — beliefs that worry is uncontrollable and damaging
*Response options: Rings true · Sometimes · Not really · Haven't noticed*

1. Once my mind starts running on something, I can't switch it off — it keeps going on its own.
2. When the thinking won't quiet down, I sense it's costing me my clarity or my edge.
3. When I've been turning something over for a while, I notice it in my body — tension that won't release, sleep that won't come.
4. Even when I try to redirect my attention, the thinking pulls me back in. The control isn't mine.
5. Some part of me worries that if the thinking keeps going at this pace, something in me is going to give.
6. Once a thought is in my system, I can't just set it aside. It stays running whether I want it there or not.

### CC — distrust of one's own cognition
*Response options: Rings true · Sometimes · Not really · Haven't noticed*

1. When I try to remember something important — a detail, a number, what was said — I don't fully trust what comes back.
2. When I try to reconstruct what happened — who said what, in what order — I'm not sure my memory has it right.
3. I sometimes question whether I actually did something I think I did — sent the message, locked the door, said the words.
4. I check things twice because part of me doesn't trust that I got it right the first time.
5. My attention slips and I lose track of what I was paying attention to — and I don't fully trust my read on what I noticed and what I missed.
6. When my mind tells me something — a recollection, a sense of what's happening — I don't take it at face value. I check against something else.

### NC — rigid control of thoughts as imperative
*Response options: Rings true · Sometimes · Not really · Haven't noticed*

1. A disciplined mind doesn't let thoughts drift wherever they want — control should be steady.
2. Some thoughts shouldn't be allowed to come up in the first place — they're evidence I'm slipping somewhere.
3. When my thinking goes where I don't want it to, that's on me. I should be able to hold the line.
4. I'd be more capable if I had tighter control over what runs through my mind.
5. Certain kinds of thinking don't belong in someone serious. When they show up, something's off.
6. If I couldn't manage what my mind is doing, I wouldn't be able to do what I need to do.

### CSC — quality of self-observation (Stillform-specific reformulation, see §6)
*Response options: Toward structure · Loops back · Both, depending · Haven't noticed*

1. When I observe my thinking running on something hard, the observation tends to move...
2. When I catch myself watching my own thoughts, the watching usually goes...
3. After I've been paying attention to my own mental patterns for a while, what I have is what comes from going...
4. When something is bothering me and I turn inward to look at how I'm processing it, the looking goes...
5. When I'm aware of my thinking, the awareness tends to move...
6. Looking at my mental patterns over time, my self-observation seems to go...

## 5 · RESULT PRESENTATION (no scores, no prevalence)

One observation per subscale. No numbers, no ranks, no good/bad. Pattern observation + research-model grounding + optional next step.

### Endorsed (rings true or sometimes on ≥4 of 6 items in a subscale)

> Pattern observed: [subscale name in Stillform language]. [One-sentence description of what's running.] This is one of the patterns Wells's metacognitive model maps. Want to add this to your Bias Profile watch list? Stillform's AI will flag it during Reframe when it shows up in your language, and the practice can shift the underlying belief over time.

Subscale-name-in-Stillform-language and the one-sentence description per subscale:

- **POS** → *Worry-as-engine*: a sense that worry is doing useful work — that rehearsing what could go wrong is what keeps you sharp or prepared.
- **NEG** → *Mind-as-mercy*: a sense that your own thinking can damage you — that worry has a cost and you're at the mercy of it.
- **CC** → *Memory-distrust*: a tendency not to trust what your own mind returns — checking twice, hedging against your own recall and attention.
- **NC** → *Mind-as-discipline*: a sense that thoughts must be held under control, and that uncontrolled or unwanted thinking is a problem to correct.
- **CSC** → handled separately below (different question shape).

### Not endorsed (responses cluster at "not really" / "haven't noticed")

> This pattern doesn't strongly run in your responses right now. Re-take this anytime; patterns can shift.

(No relief framing. No "good." Neutral observation.)

### CSC result (different shape — reports on the quality of metacognition, not a dysfunctional belief)

> Pattern observed: when you observe your own thinking, the observation tends to [loop back / move toward structure / vary depending]. This is the difference between rumination (looping without resolution) and generative metacognition (the practice Stillform builds).
>
> [If loops back] The practice is the work that converts looping into structure — that's what Reframe is for.
> [If toward structure] Your metacognition is already moving toward the work the practice is designed to build.

## 6 · THE CSC REFORMULATION (why this subscale is adapted differently)

Wells's clinical model treats Cognitive Self-Consciousness — sustained attention to one's own thinking — as part of the Cognitive Attentional Syndrome (CAS), i.e. maladaptive. But Stillform IS structured cognitive self-awareness as practice. The very tendency the MCQ-30 measures as dysfunctional on CSC is, in Stillform's architecture, the work itself.

Porting the CSC subscale verbatim would tell a user doing legitimate structured metacognitive practice that their practice is a pattern to watch — gaslighting them about their own work. So the CSC items are reworked to distinguish the two modes the clinical instrument can't separate:

- **Maladaptive CSC** — uncontrolled inward fixation, rumination loops, no structured output.
- **Generative metacognition** — structured analytical practice producing named concepts, distinguishing signals, integrating back into action.

The reworked items ask not "do you monitor your thinking" (Stillform's whole practice is yes) but "when you observe your thinking, does it move toward naming and structure, or does it loop back on itself." The science citation (Wells) still holds — Stillform measures a finer-grained version of the same construct, closer to what the user actually experiences. This is integrity-through-research in practice: faithful to the construct, adapted for the context.

## 10 · ADAPTATION CHALLENGES NOTED PER SUBSCALE (for future review)

- **NEG** — source items use dated, dramatic language ("go mad," "damage me physically"). Stillform items capture the uncontrollable-and-dangerous construct without the catastrophizing wording.
- **NC** — source items use explicit moral judgment ("weakness," "stronger person," "bad to think"). Stillform items capture the rigid-control construct without inserting Stillform's own judgment language (per USER TREATMENT principle 1).
- **CC** — most clinical-feeling subscale; source reads like memory complaints. Stillform items reframe as the felt experience of distrusting one's own mind, which is the metacognition-relevant layer.
- **POS** — most directly translatable; non-clinical adaptation straightforward.
- **CSC** — see §6. The substantial reformulation.
