# STILLFORM WORKSHOP — INSTRUMENT #4: EMOTION REGULATION

**ARA Embers LLC · spec locked May 20, 2026 · status: SPEC LOCKED, build pending**

Workshop instrument #4, adapted from the ERQ (Gross & John 2003). Capacities surface — **Settle** (regulation). Follows the capacity result-presentation pattern established by the SRIS spec (#3).

---

## 1 · WHAT THIS IS

The emotion-regulation tool of the Workshop. It measures the strategy the user already leans on to handle feeling — and the headline is that one of those strategies, **cognitive reappraisal, is exactly what Reframe does.** So ERQ reads how habitually a person already runs Stillform's core move before they ever opened the app. The other strategy, expressive suppression, is the thing Stillform explicitly is *not*. Capacities-surface instrument; feeds the growth mirror, not a chip.

---

## 2 · SOURCE RESEARCH

**Primary citation:** Gross, J. J., & John, O. P. (2003). Individual differences in two emotion regulation processes. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 85(2), 348–362.

**Structure:** 10 items, 7-point scale, two independent strategies — Cognitive Reappraisal (6 items, α ≈ .89: changing how you interpret a situation to change its emotional impact) and Expressive Suppression (4 items, α ≈ .76: inhibiting the outward display of emotion). The two are independent (high/low on each is possible). The **total score is meaningless** — only the two subscale reads matter. Reappraisal is linked to better well-being; suppression to worse affect and social outcomes. 6-item short form exists. Widely validated across community, student, and clinical samples.

**Verbatim ERQ items are NOT reproduced.** Original Stillform-voiced adaptations, faithful to each construct.

---

## 3 · ADAPTATION PRINCIPLES

Holds the USER TREATMENT principles (FRAMING_LAW v1.4 / CANON v1.9) and the capacity-surface discipline (low = room to grow, never deficit; high = usable foundation, never gold star). Plus one ERQ-specific guard:

- **Not "reappraisal good / suppression bad."** Reappraisal over-used can invalidate legitimate feeling; suppression is sometimes the right call (you don't always express everything everywhere). The result names which strategy the user leans on and what Stillform builds (the reappraisal muscle) — without moralizing suppression. The honesty is: reappraisal is the skill the practice trains; that's a statement about Stillform, not a verdict on the user.

---

## 4 · THE 10 ADAPTED ITEMS

First-person, plain; interleaved in production. **Response shape:** four-point capacity self-report — *Rarely true · Sometimes · Often · Almost always true.*

### Workshop intro
> This looks at how you already handle a feeling when it shows up — the move you reach for. There are two here. One is reinterpreting the situation so it hits differently; the other is keeping the feeling from showing. No right answer, no score — just where each sits for you. About 3 minutes.

### CR — Cognitive Reappraisal (reinterpreting to change the impact — what Reframe does)
1. "When something's getting to me, I change how I think about it to change how it feels."
2. "I handle stress by finding a different way to see the situation."
3. "When I want to feel less of a hard emotion, I rethink what's actually going on."
4. "I can shift my feeling about something by shifting my read of it."
5. "When I want to feel more of a good emotion, I think about the situation in a way that brings it up."
6. "Reframing a situation is something I do to manage how I feel about it."

### ES — Expressive Suppression (keeping the feeling from showing)
7. "I keep my emotions to myself."
8. "When I'm feeling something strong, I make sure it doesn't show."
9. "I control my feelings by not expressing them."
10. "I hold back what I'm feeling around other people."

## 5 · RESULT PRESENTATION (capacity pattern — Settle)

Read as two independent leans, no total. Per the capacity template:

- **Reappraisal high** → "You already do the core move Stillform trains — you change the read to change the feeling. That's the reappraisal muscle, and it's strong. The practice sharpens what's already there." (Foundation, not gold star.)
- **Reappraisal low** → "Reinterpreting a situation to shift how it lands isn't a move you reach for much yet — which makes it the most direct thing the practice builds. Reframe *is* this skill, rep by rep." (Room to grow.)
- **Suppression high** → named without judgment: "You tend to hold feelings in rather than let them show. That's a real strategy with real costs over time — and it's worth knowing it's your default. Stillform doesn't ask you to perform feeling; it builds the other lever, so holding-in isn't the only option you've got." (Honest about cost, no moralizing.)
- **Suppression low** → light, no inflation.

**Feeds the capacities growth mirror** (not a chip). The reappraisal line is the one that maps to Stillform's own work — a user's reappraisal lean is a natural before/after marker as Reframe builds the muscle. Internal precision read (not shown): reappraisal = the prior-updating move of the Precision Framework; ERQ measures the habitual rate of that update.

---

## 6 · THRESHOLD + ACCESS

- **Push:** proposed when a user's Reframe history shows they respond to prompts but rarely generate their own reinterpretation (low spontaneous reappraisal), or shows heavy holding-in language. Offers the look at the regulation lever.
- **Pull:** Library → Workshop. Short (10 items) — a good early take.
- **Retake:** growth mirror; tracks the reappraisal lean over time as the practice builds it.

## 7 · FREQUENCY
~3 minutes (10 items). Shortest instrument in the set.

## 8 · ADAPTATION CHALLENGES NOTED
- Resist showing a total or a "good regulation" score — only the two leans, framed per capacity discipline.
- Suppression must be named honestly (it has costs) without moralizing — the user may have good reasons in context.
- Overlap with Reframe itself: ERQ measures the skill Reframe trains, so the result can point directly at the practice ("Reframe is this skill") more concretely than any other instrument.
