Two-Pathway Regulation: Why Composure Apps Should Assess Before They Intervene

Stillform Research · April 2026

Most regulation tools — breathing apps, meditation timers, AI chatbots — make a hidden assumption: that everyone regulates the same way. Open the app, start breathing, feel better. For about half of users, this works. For the other half, it makes things worse.

The research explains why.

Two Distinct Pathways

Emotions can be generated from the "bottom up" — in response to physical sensations, body states, and autonomic arousal — or from the "top down," through cognitive appraisals, interpretations, and thought patterns. This distinction isn't theoretical. Neuroimaging studies show these pathways recruit different neural systems and respond to different intervention types.

Ochsner et al. (2009) demonstrated that top-down generated emotions are more successfully regulated by top-down strategies (cognitive reappraisal), while bottom-up emotions respond better to somatic approaches. The pathway matters.

In practice: if your emotions start as thoughts — replaying a conversation, catastrophizing tomorrow, building arguments in your head — a breathing exercise won't reach the problem. You need cognitive engagement first. Your mind needs traction before your body can settle.

If your emotions start as body states — jaw clenching, chest tightness, restless energy — talking about it won't help until the body calms down. The somatic signal needs to be addressed at its source.

The Problem With One-Size-Fits-All

Gross's process model of emotion regulation (2015) identifies five strategy families: situation selection, situation modification, attentional deployment, cognitive change, and response modulation. Of these, cognitive reappraisal (an antecedent-focused strategy) and expressive suppression (a response-focused strategy) have been most studied.

The evidence is consistent: reappraisal outperforms suppression across nearly every measure — emotional experience, physiological arousal, social outcomes, and long-term well-being. But reappraisal is a top-down strategy. For someone whose emotional response begins in the body, asking them to "reframe" before regulating physiologically is asking them to think their way out of a physical state.

Price & Hooven (2018) found that developing interoceptive awareness — the ability to notice and interpret body signals — facilitates adaptive reappraisal processes. The body informs the mind, not the other way around.

Assessment as the Foundation

Rather than defaulting every user into the same flow, regulation tools should determine which pathway a person primarily uses — and adapt accordingly. This doesn't require clinical assessment. Simple scenario-based questions can distinguish patterns:

"You're about to walk into a job interview. What do you notice first?"

If the answer is "my mind is racing through what I might be asked" — that's thought-first processing. If it's "my hands are shaking and my chest feels tight" — that's body-first. Neither is better. Both are valid. But the intervention should match.

Implications for Design

A composure system that assesses before it intervenes can route users to the right tool at the right time. Thought-first users get cognitive engagement (AI-assisted reframing) before somatic work. Body-first users get physiological regulation (breathing, body scan) before cognitive processing. Balanced users choose their entry point.

This isn't personalization for its own sake. It's the difference between an intervention that works and one that creates frustration — which, for someone already in distress, compounds the problem.

The Metacognitive Shift

Beyond matching tools to pathways, the assessment itself has therapeutic value. By asking a person to notice how they process — not just what they feel — you're training metacognition. The ability to observe your own system, rather than be consumed by it, is the foundation of every evidence-based regulation strategy from CBT to Somatic Experiencing.

The goal isn't to make people dependent on a tool. It's to build a skill they eventually own. The less they need the app, the more it's working.
